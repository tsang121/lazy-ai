How to Get Claude to Write Like a Professional

Claudius 200K context window is unmatched. Learn the techniques to produce long-form content that reads naturally and engages readers.

AI Writing | 7 min read | 2025-02-12

Claude's 200K context window makes it the king of long-form content. But raw power needs direction. Here is how to get Claude to write like a seasoned professional.

1. Give Claude a Writing Role

Before you ask for content, establish who is writing:

"You are a senior technology journalist who has written for Wired and The Verge. Your style is conversational yet authoritative."

2. Define Your Target Reader

Great writing serves a specific audience:

"Write for a 35-year-old small business owner who is comfortable with technology but not a developer. They need practical advice, not technical jargon."

3. Provide Examples of Your Voice

Claude can match your style:

"Here is a sample of my writing: [paste 2-3 paragraphs]. Write the rest of this article in the same voice and tone."

4. Use Section-by-Section Prompts

For long content, guide Claude step by step:

"First, write a compelling introduction (150 words) that hooks the reader. Then wait for my approval before continuing to the main body."

5. Ask for Transitions

Transitions make or break long-form content:

"After each section, include a 2-sentence transition that smoothly leads into the next topic."

6. Specify Paragraph Length

Web readers need scannable content:

"Keep paragraphs to 3-4 sentences maximum. Use shorter paragraphs for key points."

7. Request Inline Subheadings

Break up long text visually:

"Every 300 words, add an H3 subheading that summarizes what follows."

8. Ask for Questions to Engage

Rhetorical questions keep readers involved:

"Include 2-3 thought-provoking questions throughout the article to engage the reader."

9. Give Claude an Ending Template

Strong conclusions leave impressions:

"End with: 1) A 2-sentence summary, 2) One actionable takeaway, 3) A question that invites comments."

10. Edit in Iterations

First draft is never final:

"Now revise for: (1) Remove redundancy, (2) Strengthen verbs, (3) Add one surprising fact."

Pro Tip: The Research Sandwich

Get Claude to cite sources mid-article:

"Before writing each section, first list 3 relevant statistics or studies I could mention. Then write the section incorporating them naturally."
